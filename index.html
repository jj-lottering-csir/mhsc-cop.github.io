INTRODUCTION TO THE OPERATIONAL MANUAL

  1. The Mining Occupational Health Advisory Committee (MOHAC) of the Mine Health and Safety Council (MHSC) revised the current “Guideline for the compilation of a mandatory COP for an occupational health programme on personal exposure to airborne pollutants” that was published by the DMR in 2002. The result was the revised “Guideline for the Compilation of a Mandatory Code of Practice: Occupational Health Programme on Personal Exposures to Airborne Pollutants” (DMR 16/3/2/4-A1 effective 30 April 2018) (Herein referred to as the “Guideline”)
  2. A decision was made by MOHAC to issue an Operations Manual with the revised guideline. The aim of the operations manual is to assist the end-user with the development and implementation of the Code of Practice. The following sections of the operations manual are in line with the sections of the guideline and provides explanations, references and examples (where relevant) for that section. The aim of the operations manual is to standardise practices across the South African Mining Industry (SAMI).
  3. The Operational Manual is supplementary to the Guideline and the content should not be considered as mandatory requirements. The Operational Manual was developed to provide clarity on the understanding and interpretation of the Guideline.
  4. The Operational Manual does not train the end-user on the disciplines of Occupational Hygiene, Risk Assessment, Statistical Data Analysis and Mine Ventilation. The assumption is that a Section 12(1) Appointee has the necessary qualifications, knowledge and experience related to these disciplines.
  5. The Operational Manual was developed in consultation with all stakeholders (State, Government and Labour) to align and standardise practices in the SAMI.

HOW TO USE THE OPERATIONAL MANUAL

The Operational Manual consists of two parts:

Part 1: Information in normal text is the content as it appears in the Guideline and the information was included in the Operational Manual for ease reference.

Part 2: The Operational Manual content is shown within a border and provides the explanation and/or an example that relates to the Guideline requirement. The Operational Manual content is for information purposes.

1. FOREWORD

The Foreword of the Guideline has minor editorial changes to take the name change of the DMR (from the then DME referenced in the previous version) and the name change of the Guideline itself into account.

The Guideline was issued by the Department of Mineral Resources (DMR) in 2018 and for this reason the Operational Manual maintains the reference to the DMR even though there was a name change to the Department during 2019.

Information is provided to the user on the undertaking of the Occupational Hygiene Programme.

  1. The Commission of Inquiry into Safety and Health in the Mining Industry chaired by the Honourable Mr. Justice R N Leon identified occupational health as one of the four major issues affecting occupational health and safety in the mining industry.
  2. To address this issue a tripartite sub-committee was established under the auspices of the Mining Occupational Health Advisory Committee (MOHAC). MOHAC found it necessary to address this issue in that a guideline for a mandatory COP for Airborne Pollutants is drafted.
  3. Significant risks to health exist in mining. In order to protect, monitor and promote employees’ health status, an occupational health programme is required where exposure to such significant risks occur. MOHAC considered it appropriate to prepare a guideline covering both occupational hygiene and medical surveillance to ensure compliance and uniform standards.
  4. Where the employer’s risk assessment indicates a need to establish and maintain either a system of occupational hygiene measurements or a system of medical surveillance, or where either such system is required by regulation, the employer must prepare and implement a COP based on this guideline.
  5. This guideline assists employers with the establishment of an Occupational Health Programme but does not stipulate specific requirements for specific circumstances. It sets out a basic system for managing risks to health. The first component of any management system is finding out what the situation is, the second is deciding what to do about it.
  6. This guideline replaces the “Guideline for the compilation of a mandatory COP for an occupational health programme on personal exposure to airborne pollutants with reference to DME 16/3/2/4-A1” published by the Department of Mineral Resources (DMR) in 2002.

2. LEGAL STATUS OF GUIDELINE AND CODE OF PRACTICES

In accordance with section 9(2) of the Mine Health and Safety Act (MHSA) an employer must prepare and implement a COP on any matter affecting the health and safety of employees and other persons who may be directly affected by activities at the mine if the Chief Inspector of Mines (CIOM) requires it. These COPs must comply with any relevant guidelines issued by the CIOM [section 9(3) MHSA]. Failure by the employer to prepare or implement a COP in compliance with this guideline is a breach of the MHSA.

The Mine Health and Safety Act (MHSA) was drafted as a result of the inquiry into all aspects of legal regulation of health and safety in the mining industry by the Leon Commission. Under section 49 of the MHSA the Chief Inspector of Mines (CIOM) is required to issue guidelines on any matters affecting the health and safety of workers at mines. Such guidelines are issued in consultation with the MHSC. Codes of practices (COPs) are then drawn-up by each mine in accordance with these guidelines.

3. OBJECTIVES OF THE GUIDELINE

The objectives of the Guideline are stipulated in this section.

  1. The objective of this guideline is to enable the employer at every mine to compile a COP, which, if properly implemented and complied with, would protect and improve the health of employees at the mine by monitoring and reducing their exposure to airborne pollutants.

    The Risk Assessment should reflect if the health of employees is indeed protected. Implementation of the COP should be evaluated to assess if the health of employees is protected through the implementation of the COP activities.

  2. This guideline provides guidance of a general nature on the required format and content for the COP and details sufficient technical background to enable the drafting committee at the mine to prepare a comprehensive and practical COP for the mine. It sets out the two components of an Occupational Health Programme namely:
    1. Occupational Hygiene; and
    2. Medical Surveillance
  3. Where an employer is required, in terms of Regulation 9.2(2) or in terms of its risk assessment, to establish and maintain a system of occupational hygiene measurements in respect of airborne pollutants, this guideline should assist the employer in doing so.

4. DEFINITIONS AND ACRONYMS

This section clarifies definitions and acronyms that are used throughout the guideline. When developing a COP there should be a section for definitions, terminology or abbreviations that are often used on the mine as this is useful to the reader who may not be familiar with such terms.

It is important to note the following:
It is customary to use standard terms and definitions to avoid variable interpretations by the reader. Please refer to ISO 18158 “Workplace Air – Terminology” for all definitions related to Workplace Air. ISO 18158 specifies terms and definitions that are related to the Assessment of Workplace Exposure to Chemical and Biological Agents. These are either general terms or are specific to physical and chemical processes of air sampling, the analytical method or method performance. The terms in ISO 18158 are those that have been identified as being fundamental because their definition is necessary to avoid ambiguity and ensure consistency of use. ISO 18158 is applicable to all International Standards, Technical Reports, Technical Specifications, and Guides related to Workplace Air Quality. Some ISO 18158 definitions are provided in this manual for further information.

In this guideline for a COP, unless the context otherwise indicates:

“Airborne pollutant” means any substance in the air that is harmful to health, including dust, fumes, aerosols, gases, fibres, vapours or mists;

It is important that the nature of the airborne pollutant is identified and clearly defined as this will impact on the measuring procedure employed and the route of entry for exposure.

    ISO 18158 definitions:
  • air pollutant: material emitted into the atmosphere either by human activity or natural processes and adversely affecting humans or the environment
  • airborne dust: finely divided matter, in solid form, dispersed in air
  • airborne particles: fine matter, in solid or liquid form, dispersed in air
  • biological agent: one of a number of agents such as bacteria, viruses, fungi and other micro-organisms or parts of them and their associated toxins, including those which have been genetically modified, cell cultures or endoparasites which are potentially hazardous to human health (Dusts of organic origin, for example cotton dust, flour dust and wood dust, are not considered to be biological agents and are therefore not covered by this definition);
  • chemical agent: chemical element or compound on its own or admixed as it occurs in the natural state or as produced, used, or released, including release as waste, by any work activity, whether or not produced intentionally and whether or not placed on the market
  • fume: aerosol of solid particles, usually from metallurgical processes, generated by condensation from the gaseous state, generally after volatilization from melted substances and often accompanied by chemical reactions such as oxidation
  • total airborne particles: airborne particles present in a given volume of air
  • vapour: gas phase of a substance in a state of equilibrium or disturbed equilibrium with the same substance in a liquid or solid state below its boiling or sublimation point

“analysis methodology” means analysis techniques used to quantify a pollutant collected on or in sampling media (e.g. gas chromatography/mass spectrometry);

ISO 18158 definition:

analysis: all operations carried out after sample preparation to determine the amount or concentration of the analyte(s) of interest present in the sample

“CIOM” means Chief Inspector of Mines;
“COP” means Code of Practice;

A COP is a document that outlines how the Occupational Health Programme on Personal Exposures to Airborne Pollutants is implemented at the mine in order to protect the health of employees and comply with legislation. The COP should remain current and written in such a manner to ensure consistent implementation.

“DMR” means the Department of Mineral Resources;

Although there was a name change recently, the DMR issued the latest version of the Guideline. For this reason, all references in this document to the department will still be DMR.

“exposure” means the subjection of a person to an airborne pollutant during employment through any route of entry (e.g. inhalation, ingestion, skin contact or absorption);

Consideration should be given on employees that perform similar jobs or activities. Some aspects to consider when establishing HEGs (or SEGs) are the process, the job itself, the task and the airborne pollutant.

ISO 18158 definition:
➢ exposure: [inhalation] situation in which a chemical agent or biological agent is present in the air that is inhaled by a person

“homogeneous exposure group (HEG)” means a group of employees whose exposures to a hazardous agent have been determined to be statistically similar enough that, by monitoring a small number of individuals in the group, the exposures of the remaining workers can be defined;

The definition was revised to emphasise that HEGs should be classified based on statistical data. Some international documents refer to similar exposure groups (SEG).

BOHS/NVVA (2011): SEGs are defined as “A group of workers having the same general exposure profile for an agent because of the similarity of the materials and processes with which they work, and the similarity of the way they perform the task(s).” Mulhausen and Damiano (2006). These SEGs can be established either by observing the work activity or by using a "sampling approach" that consists of creating the SEGs on the basis of a statistical analysis of the exposure data (Mulhausen and Damiano (1998)).

EN 689 (1996): The preferred approach is to subdivide the exposed population into homogeneous groups with respect to exposure. The variability of exposure levels is smaller for well-defined groups than for the exposed workforce as a whole. Thus, where a group of workers is performing identical or similar tasks at the same place and has a similar exposure, sampling such as representative of the group may be carried out within that group.

“MOHAC” means Mining Occupational Health Advisory Committee;

“monitoring” means the repetitive and continued observation, measurement, and evaluation of health and/or environmental or technical data, according to prearranged schedules, using nationally or internationally acceptable methodologies;

Acceptable methodologies are those recognised by national or international standardisation bodies or the scientific community. The South African Bureau of Standards (SABS) publishes South African National Standards (SANS). International methods may be used in the absence of SANS. Examples of international organisation are the International Standards Organisation (ISO) and the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM). Some institutions have developed methods over the years that are widely recognised and implemented, for example methods by the Health and Safety Executive or the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH).

ISO 18158 definition:
➢ measuring procedure/measurement procedure/measurement method: set of operations described specifically for the sampling and analysis of chemical or biological agents in air (Usually includes preparation for sampling, performance of sampling, transportation and storage, and sample preparation for analysis and performance of analysis).

Measuring is the sampling and analysis of the pollutant to determine exposure. Sampling is the act of capturing the pollutant in the workplace. The analysis step is the characterisation and quantification of the pollutant that was captured. Measuring is not a once-off act but is a ongoing process over time to determine if the controls that were implemented, are effective.

“MHSA” means Mine Health and Safety Act, 1996 (Act No.29 of 1996) as amended;

Definition for mixtures: The Guideline does not specify a definition for “mixtures”. However, for the purpose of drafting a COP it is important to have internal definitions or descriptions for mixtures based on the context as it applies to the different workplaces. Guidance on certain terms regarding mixtures is provided below:
•  Aggregate exposure: same chemical from multiple sources and/or multiple pathways
•  Combined exposure to multiple chemicals: multiple chemicals through a single or multiple source
•  Assessment group: chemicals grouped together for evaluation as combined exposure.
•  Chemical mixture: combined exposure to multiple chemicals regardless of source; may or may not be identified. May contribute to joint toxicity in a target population.
•  Intentional mixtures: manufactured products
Different methodologies for assessing exposure to multiple compounds are discussed in a publication by Bopp et al (2018).

“NIOSH” means the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (United States);

“qualitative” means observations or information characterised by measurement on a categorical scale (i.e. dichotomous or nominal scale) or, if the categories are ordered, an ordinal scale e.g. “low”, “medium”, “high”.

“Qualitative” will be relevant to a walk-through survey where observations are made, and an initial categorisation of exposure is made based on theoretical assumptions.

“quantitative” means data in numerical quantities such as continuous measurements or counts e.g. percentile, rates.

“Quantitative” refers to actual measurements that were taken to quantify the exposure. The outcomes of the quantitative measures are compared with the qualitative and used to evaluate future assessments.

“occupational exposure limit (OEL)” means the TWA concentration for an 8-hour work day and a 40 hour work week to which nearly all workers may be repeatedly exposed without adverse health effects;

It is important to note that an OEL is not the same as the “Milestone Targets”. Milestones refer to the agreement that was signed between the State, Industry and Labour at the 2014 Occupational Health and Safety Summit. The milestone relevant to Airborne Pollutants is: “By December 2024, 95% of all exposure measurement results will be below the milestone level for respirable crystalline silica of 0.05 mg/m3 (these results are individual results and not average results).” Milestones provide a measure for all stakeholders to monitor progress on the journey to achieve zero harm. The milestone is not a legislative requirement however, the OEL’s for Airborne Pollutants are contained in one of the regulations of the Mine Health and Safety Act.

“sampling cycle” means the planned sampling programme for the year, which must terminate at the end of each calendar year;

The sampling cycle refers to the 12-month period 1 January to 31 December for which the monitoring and sampling strategy is valid.

“TWA” means Time Weighted Average; and “90th percentile” means the statistical value of exposure data which must be used to determine when HEG’s need to be re-classified, calculated either by:

  • using Microsoft Excel programme (percentile function); or
  • first placing all sample results in order from the lowest concentration to the highest concentration (i.e., concentration of specific contaminants). Next, assign each sample result a number, starting with the number 1 for the lowest concentration result up to the highest concentration being given the number equal to the total number of samples collected in that HEG. Multiply the total number of samples collected by 0.9. The sample result with the number corresponding to this calculated value is the 90th percentile.

It is important to note that annual HEGs are re-classified based on the 90th percentile of the pollutant concentration or the 90th percentile of the Air Quality Index (AQI) (whichever is greater). Refer to the Reporting Tutorial for guidance on how to calculate and use this percentile value.
The quarterly HEGs are classified based on the 90th percentile of pollutant concentration or AQI (whichever is greater). Refer to the Tutorials for guidance on how to calculate and use this percentile value.

5.SCOPE

  1. Regulation 9.2(2) requires that a system of occupational hygiene measurements on personal exposure to airborne pollutants must be prepared and implemented when the results of the risk assessment conducted has identified that the following hazard limits prevail:
    1. Particulates ≥ 1/10 (10%) of the OEL
    2. Gases ≥ ½ (50%) of the OEL
    Pollutants that pose a health risk according to the scope of the Guideline are included in the Occupational Health Programme. Please note that 5.1.2 should also include “Vapours”
  2. Where there is no statutory determined OEL for a particular airborne pollutant the risk assessment conducted should determine how the pollutant must be addressed in the COP.
    The COP should describe how the pollutants identified as per 5.2 are monitored and controlled even though it is not a requirement to report personal exposure to the DMR. For example, diesel exhaust was classified as a human carcinogen but there is no established OEL in South Africa yet. Personal exposure to diesel particulate matter (DPM) (used as the marker for diesel exhaust) should be controlled and monitored and the information should be readily available on request by the DMR.
  3. This guideline is aimed at assisting employers to draft a COP for an Occupational Health Programme which measures occupational exposures to airborne pollutants aimed at ensuring compliance with OELs, protecting worker health and linking these exposure results to employee medical records.
    Refer to section 8.4.1.2 of this document for guidance on how to do linkage between exposure results and employee medical records.

PART B: AUTHORS GUIDE

  1. The COP must, where possible, follow the sequence laid out in Part C “Format and Content of the COP”. The pages as well as the chapters and sections must be numbered, where possible, to facilitate cross-referencing. Wording must be unambiguous and concise.
    It is important to note that the COP should not be a copy of the Guideline. The COP should clearly state how the requirements of the Guideline are addressed. There should then be sufficient evidence to show that the COP is implemented. For ease of assessment by the Inspector, the COP numbering should agree with those of the Guideline.
  2. It should be indicated in the COP and on each annexure to the COP whether:
    1. The annexure forms part of the COP and must be complied with or incorporated in the COP or whether aspects thereof must be complied with or incorporated in the COP; or
    2. The annexure is merely attached as information for consideration in the preparation of the COP (i.e. compliance is discretionary).
    Clearly distinguish in the COP which additional annexures are mandatory and which are for information purposes only. For example a register of reference documents may be for information purposes only but an annexure that contains the Pollutant Codes is mandatory.
  3. When annexures are used the numbering should be preceded by the letter allocated to that particular annex and the numbering should start at one (1) again. (e.g. 1, 2, 3, A1, A2, A3…).
  4. Whenever possible illustrations, tables, graphs and the like, should be used to avoid long descriptions and/or explanations.
    It may be easier to describe a process using a conceptual diagram or a figure and just refer to another comprehensive document (or COP). Include the reference to the comprehensive document in the reference register and ensure that this document is readily available for review
  5. When reference has been made in the text to publications or reports, references to these sources must be included in the text as footnotes or side notes as well as in a separate bibliography.
    These references can be in the form of journal articles, conference papers, company reports, regulatory instructions etc. It is important that any document that is referenced is readily available in a hard or soft copy. Maintain a register of these documents as one of the annexures to the COP.

5. General Information

The general information should already give the reader an indication of the anticipated airborne pollutants exposures. When the initial, qualitative risk assessment is done, this section may be useful.

General relevant information relating to the mine must be stated in this section of the COP, which must include at least the following:

  1. A brief description of the mine and its location;

    Provide context to the reader and the end-user on the mine, its location and perhaps the extended location based on mining operations. This section provides institutional memory on how exposures may change over time.

  2. The commodities produced;

    Describe the commodities produced and align this section with the content of Annexure A. Amend this section as new or different ore bodies are mined over time that may give rise to changes in personal exposures.

  3. The mining method or combination of methods used at the mine must be listed. This section must discuss the degree of mechanisation, taking care to identify the potential sources of pollutants, and possible pathways of exposure and also possible exposure scenarios;

    Describe the mining methods and list the potential, associated airborne pollutants. This information may be presented in a table and may be combined with the information from 5.4.

  4. The general controls in place to prevent exposure to airborne pollutants including ventilation arrangements;

    As explained in 5.3 it may be worth presenting the controls with the mining methods and associated pollutants in one table. Provide a high-level overview of the type of controls in use with examples of each. It is not required to describe the entire ventilation system but only what is applicable to airborne pollutant control.

  5. Other related COPs and management standards must be reviewed concurrently in order to avoid conflict of requirements as laid down by the employer. The objective would be to have an integrated system; and

    Describe which department or designation is responsible for the comparison of the organisation’s COPs and standards (e.g. legal or quality department). Describe how often these documents are reviewed to assess for any conflicts. For example, supply chain may have a policy that prescribes the main criteria for diesel fuel is the price. However, this policy may be in direct conflict with the control measure of acquiring low sulphur fuel.

  6. The unique features of the mine that have a bearing on this COP and cross-reference them to the risk assessment conducted.

    In addition to the above points it is important to describe any aspects that have not been covered but may have an impact on the Occupational Health Programme. For example, the mine may have a geological feature that is unusual for the type of commodity mine.

  2. Components of an Occupational Health Programme
      The Occupational Health Programme has two components namely:
    1. Occupational Hygiene; and
    2. Medical Surveillance
  3. Occupational Hygiene Programme
      In order to prepare the Occupational Hygiene for the mine, the COP should cover the following steps:
    • Step 1 - Risk Assessment
    • Step 2 - Determination of HEGs
    • Step 3 - Sampling and Analysis Methodology and Quality Control
    • Step 4 - Personal Exposure Monitoring
    • Step 5 - Linking of Occupational Exposure
    • Step 6 - Hierarchy of control measures
    • Step 7 - Reporting and Recording
    1. Risk assessment

      The COP should set out measures to ensure that a qualitative and quantitative risk assessment process is followed and takes into account all the factors influencing the health of employees.

      Qualitative risk assessment: Process of obtaining an initial, high-level overview of the risk of exposure. Methods that are employed are walk-through surveys, screening or semi-quantitative measurements and visual observation. Once a high-level risk assessment is completed, a quantitative risk assessment can be done.

      Quantitative risk assessment: Process of quantifying or measuring the risk more accurately. The first time a quantitative risk assessment is carried out, it is usually called the “Baseline Risk Assessment (BRA)”. This assessment is used as the starting point at which improvements are made and controls are implemented.

      Continuous monitoring and assessment are the ongoing, quantitative assessment of employee exposure according to the sampling plan for the year. Monitoring includes the sampling (capture) and analysis (characterisation) of a pollutant.

      1. Baseline Risk Assessment

        At the initial commencement of a system of occupational hygiene measurements, as contemplated in section 12(2) of the MHSA, a baseline risk assessment is to be conducted to assess exposure to airborne pollutants.

        It is compulsory to conduct a Baseline Risk Assessment (BRA) and maintain evidence of the BRA and not only describe the process. The baseline is the quantitative risk assessment where accurate concentrations are determined, and a specific risk value is assigned to each significant pollutant (i.e. pollutants in concentrations above the specified limits as per the scope of the guideline)

        It is important to note the following:

        Align the occupational hygiene risk assessment with the safety risk assessment where possible. The intent should be to standardise practices within an organisation. Table 3 is an example of such a risk matrix.

      2. The risk assessment must be described with reference to:
        • (a) Any airborne pollutant/s to which employees are being exposed to (refer to Chapter 22. of the MHSA Airborne pollutants occupational exposure limits);

          Firstly, identify all the pollutants that employees are exposed to in the workplace. Secondly, identify those pollutants that are present in concentrations greater than 10% of the OEL (particulates) or greater than 50% of the OEL (gases and vapours). These pollutants are considered to be pollutants that have a significant risk to the health of employees and should be closely controlled and monitored

        • (b) The route of entry (where applicable i.e. inhalation, absorption, ingestion etc.) and health effects that these individual airborne pollutants can have on employees;

          It is important to know the route of entry as this will impact on the nature of controls and the type personal protective equipment that will be implemented, if necessary

          The NIOSH pocket guide for Chemical Hazards provides useful information on the route of entry: http://www.cdc.gov/niosh/npg/search.html. An example from the pocket guide for manganese particulates is shown below:

          • Exposure Routes: inhalation, ingestion
          • Symptoms: Manganism; asthenia, insomnia, mental confusion; metal fume fever: dry throat, cough, chest tightness, dyspnea (breathing difficulty), rales, flu-like fever; low-back pain; vomiting; malaise (vague feeling of discomfort); lassitude (weakness, exhaustion); kidney damage
          • Target Organs: respiratory system, central nervous system, blood, kidneys
        • (c) Where such pollutants may be present, e.g. welding bay, spray painting booth, battery charging stations, stope face, development end etc.;

          Note the actual source or origin of the airborne pollutant. A combination of pollutants can originate from the same workplace e.g. diesel emissions contain various gases, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH) and diesel particulate matter (DPM). In processing plants, some raw materials can combine to form additional airborne pollutants.

        • (d) The airborne nature of those pollutants identified, e.g. gases, fumes, vapour mists, fibres, dusts etc.;

          It is important to note that different forms of the pollutant may have different OELs and Pollutant Codes (refer to Annexure A):

          The nature of the pollutant will also determine the sampling method e.g. gravimetric sampling using a filter and cassette for particles as opposed to sorbent tubes for gases. The COP should describe how pollutants that do not have OELs are dealt with such as diesel particulate matter (DPM).

        • (e) the nature of the key workplace operations and activities that pose the greatest potential for exposure with identified airborne pollutants;

          Take note of the operation or activities that give rise to the presence of exposures to the pollutant e.g. drilling causes dust. Diesel engine operation results in diesel engine emissions being produced; however, the pollutant concentration will vary based on the operation of the vehicle (i.e. idling vs. operating under load). The ventilation system might disperse the pollutant to other areas and increase employee exposure in other areas. During the risk assessment the different operations or activities should be assessed to determine which exposure condition poses the greatest risk to the employee.

        • (f) The occupations and number of employees who are being exposed to airborne pollutants;

          The risk assessment should be done in line with employees and their occupation i.e. employees who are at risk. The risk assessment is not limited to occupations in Annexure A and should include the occupations that are not listed in Annexure.

          It is important to note the following:

          The proximity of the employee to the source and the time spent in this proximity should be considered when assessing the risk of exposure. Proximity and duration are easy to determine for operators directly at the source for the full shift. However, for roving personnel these two parameters should be well defined.

        • (g) The pattern, i.e. intermittent, continuous etc., duration and frequency of employee exposure to the airborne pollutants identified;
            Below are some examples of how to define the pattern of exposure:
          • Continuous (chronic, repeated, uninterrupted) exposure may be defined as a full working shift i.e. 8 hours per day, 5 days per week or 40 hours per week; depending on how the shift cycles are arranged.
          • Periodic exposure may be defined in relation to the nature of the occupation e.g. exposures to welding fumes occur for approximately 10 minutes every hour during a full shift. This type of exposure may be relatively predictive and may occur at fixed intervals.
          • Intermittent exposure may be defined as random occurrences of exposure that are difficult to predict.
        • (h) The actual exposure levels measured compared to occupational exposure limits;

          Historical data may be used as part of the risk assessment such as measurements that were taken as part of the sampling schedule.

          When measuring the actual exposure levels the assessment must be representative of employee exposure for each shift. Different shifts may have different exposures due to the manner in which operations are carried out or to the extent that control measures are effective during the shift. It shouldn’t be assumed that all shifts have the same exposure levels unless sufficient statistical evidence supports such an assumption.

          The measuring of the pollutant concentration is carried out in the breathing zone of the employee. The measurement result that is obtained, with an established and acceptable uncertainty, is compared to the national OEL or international limits for the pollutant in question.

          Sampling size: The sample size for the risk assessment will depend on the exposure levels of the pollutants. The guideline does not specify the sample size for a risk assessment.

          It is important to note the following:

          The qualitative risk assessment provides an indication of where samples need to be taken for the quantitative risk assessment. More samples will be required for the risk assessment when qualitative exposure results are equal to or above the OEL than for qualitative results below 50% of the OEL. As a general rule, EN 689 recommends that at least 1 in 10 employees are measured in a HEG.

        • (i) The control measures in place, i.e. substitution, engineering, administration, personal protective equipment etc., the additional control measures required to be instituted in order to reduce or maintain exposures to below the occupational exposure limits, and if applicable the planned programme of implementation;

          The risk assessment must describe the control measures that are already in place for each pollutant, which additional control measures should be implemented. The efficiency of control measures may be assessed using direct-reading instruments and the quantitative assessment. Examples of control measures include air-filtration units, water and chemical sprays systems, haul road dust control system, local exhaust ventilation systems etc. PPE should not be considered as a permanent control system. It should at most only be used while rectifying existing controls or while implementing new controls. Refer to Table 2 for an example.

        • (j) The frequency of any ongoing monitoring to assess the effectiveness of the controls mentioned above; and

          During the risk assessment describe how frequently the effectiveness of the controls will be assessed and which methodology will be used for such an assessment e.g. personal, area or direct source sampling or any other suitable tests.

        • (k) The relevant safety data sheets as contemplated in section 21(4) (a), (b) and (c) of the MHSA.

          Safety data sheets (formerly known as material safety data sheets) should be available for raw materials or products used in the processing of mineral ore. For mineral ore itself such information will not be available. However, geologists and/or chemical engineers may provide information that will be sufficient for the purposes of a risk assessment. Alternatively, bulk ore samples can be sent for qualitative analysis to a testing laboratory and the results may be useful during the risk assessment.

      1. HEGs should be identified for purposes of personal exposure monitoring. The baseline risk assessment will enable the identification of HEGs, which will be established as follows:

        It is impractical to assess the personal exposure of every single employee. For this reason, the employee population is divided into HEGs (or sometimes referred to as Similar Exposure Groups (SEGs)). However, it is critical that the HEGs are representative of the individual exposure, within the allowable limits of between-worker variability. The baseline risk assessment informs the HEG determination.

        The objective of this COP is to describe a programme that must protect the health of the employees. The objective should not be to do meaningless sampling, but sampling should be representative of the exposure profile. The employer is required to provide the necessary resources for the continued sampling according to Section 7 of the Mine Health and Safety Act.

        For example, in Europe there is more emphasis on the Risk Assessment than on the Continuous Monitoring. If the Risk Assessment is comprehensive, then better and more effective controls can be implemented. The Continuous Monitoring should then confirm the effectiveness of the control and not be used as a reactive measure.

        The sub-division of the mine into sampling areas (e.g. Surface = Sampling Area 1, Underground Section A = Sampling Area 2, Underground Section B = Sampling Area 3, Underground Section C = Sampling Area 4, etc).

        Step 2

        At underground mines sampling areas should be subdivided into ventilation districts which are areas of a mine, ventilated independently from other areas with a common dedicated intake and return airways. Any airborne contaminants released in a ventilation district will only affect that particular district and does not circulate through other areas of the mine where people may be exposed.

          In order for an area to be classified as a ventilation district it must comply with:
        • Ventilated independently from other areas.
        • Independent intake and return airways.
        • Does not contaminate other areas.

        It is important to note the following: Some sampling areas may have its own “sub-intake” and “sub-return” ventilation compared to the main ventilation district. For example, the intake air for a Continuous Miner Section is drawn from the main ventilation district, polluted during operation and then the return from this section contributes to the main ventilation district of the next or another ventilation district.

        When pollutants that have an additive effect have been identified in an Activity Area where these effects are additive their combined effects must be used when classifying the Activity Area into their respective HEG’s, the following formula must be used to assess the additive effect.

        C1/ L1 + C2/L2 + C3/L3…. for compliance the combined effect should be < 1

        where C1, C2 etc are the concentrations of constituents in air and L1, L2 etc are the corresponding OEL’s.

        The combined effect described in Step 7 refers to the Air Quality Index (AQI) that appears on the reporting form for pollutants that have an additive effect.

        It is important that additive effects of pollutants are discussed with the relevant Occupational Medical Practitioner, Epidemiologist and Toxicologist. The decision and reasoning should be clearly documented for future reference as the combined effect should never be assumed.

        Brouwer and Rees (2016) commented on this approach in their letter to the editor of the Occupational Health Southern Africa Journal. The AQI formula assumes that all the pollutants in the mixture have the same action and contributes to the toxicity of the mixture in the ratio present. However, the solubility and size distribution of particles are different, and they tend to deposit in different areas of the respiratory system; or be removed by the body’s mechanisms. Non-soluble particles deposit in the respiratory system, end up in the lungs, but may be transferred to other organs in the body. OELs were derived with a specific target organ in mind because pollutants have a localised toxicity such as on the lungs, brain and kidney. When insolubility and localised toxicity are considered, then additive effects may be assumed for the purposes of the risk assessment and exposure monitoring.

        NOTE: The OEL’s for pollutants listed in Schedule 22.9(2)(a) of the MHSA are for single compounds or for pollutants containing a common entity. Employees are however frequently subjected to mixed exposures. It is therefore necessary to take into account the possible additive or synergistic effects of these pollutants. Due to the difficulty in assessing if the effects of a mixture of pollutants are either additive or synergistic, the requirements of this COP will be to treat all such cases as if they are additive. With all types of mixed exposures, it is essential that the classification band category assessment should be based on the concentrations of each of the constituents in air to which employees are exposed.

        Table C1 in Annexure C depicts exposures to the various hazards that can be considered as being additive or synergistic. Annexure C is mandatory and must be complied with.

        Table C1 can be found in Annexure D (page 81) and this Annexure is for information purposes only. Table C1 should be used as a guideline as there is no scientific basis for this table. Additive and synergistic effects cannot be assumed because pollutants target the same organ because the biological processes are more complicated.

          Before assigning additive and synergistic effects to a mixture of pollutants one should do the following first:
        • Define “mixtures of pollutants” for your operation;
        • Consult with the relevant Occupational Medical Practitioner, Epidemiologists and Toxicologists to evaluate the mixtures to assess which mixtures are truly additive and/or synergistic.
        • Other occupational exposures such as noise, vibration and heat may have to be considered as having a synergistic effect with airborne pollutants e.g. exposure to certain metals and noise. In these instances, a different approach may be required.

      2. The COP should address a sampling strategy that includes a monitoring schedule for each HEG. The sampling cycle and records thereof should be kept as required by law. (See for example sections 12(3) and 15(2)(b) of the MHSA.)

        A sampling strategy is an approach to measure the exposure of a pre-defined number of employees in each HEG over a period of 12 months. This strategy should be designed in such a way so as to provide sufficient evidence to show compliance with national OEL’s or the relevant international limit value. This strategy includes all information related to the HEG classification, number of individuals in a HEG, the number of samples to be taken from a HEG (depending on the exposure concentration of the pollutant) etc.

        3. Sampling methodology
        • (a) The COP should address for each pollutant identified, an appropriate sampling methodology selected and implemented. (Refer to Annexure E: Background information on particulates; sampling and analysis methodology. Annexure E is for information purposes only.)
        • (b) The COP should put measures in place to ensure that a quality control programme for the sampling methodology is developed and implemented
            NOTE:
          • (1) Refer to Annex F - Quality assurance. Annex F is for information purposes only.
          • (2) The relevant methodology chosen for each pollutant identified must be stated in the COP.

          ISO 20581 “Workplace exposure — General requirements for the performance of procedures for the measurement of chemical agents” (based on EN 482) may be useful to the user for measuring exposure to airborne pollutants in general. Section 8.3.2.1 Step 4 has references to internationally recognised methodologies that include sampling and analysis information. It is important to determine the nature of the pollutant and how sampling should be done e.g. metal fumes are not sampled in the same way as gases.

          It is recommended that a Chain-of-Custody is implemented as part of the quality control of sampling. It is a set of written records or a register of the individuals or facilities who take ownership of a sample during the entire process (i.e. blank preparation to chemical analysis result). During each step of the process the person taking custody of the sample is responsible for the protection of the integrity of the sample. This is especially important when sampling equipment changes hands during the process.

          For example, the gravimetric sampling train is prepared by the weighing facility → given over to the Pump Issuer → sampling train given to the employee being assessed → employee carries sampling train (or leaves it unattended) → sampling train returned to Pump Issuer → Sampling train returned to weighing facility. This is a simple example with only a few steps, however, in reality the entire sampling process may consist of a multitude of steps. Each step contributes some variability that may impact on the outcome of the exposure assessment.

          The aim will be to limit variables as far as possible in this process. It may be more valuable to have fewer samples where the quality can be guaranteed, then hundreds of samples with questionable integrity.

      4. Chemical analysis methodology
          The COP should address the following points:
        • (a) For each pollutant identified, an appropriate chemical analysis methodology, which complies with internationally accepted good practice, should be selected and implemented. All the selected chemical analytical methods should be listed in the COP. e.g. NIOSH methods for Silica (crystalline respirable): NIOSH 7500 (XRD); NIOSH 7602 (IR).

          Chemical analysis is laboratory testing specific to the pollutant and excludes gravimetric weighing where the collective of total dust is measured. Please refer to Section 8.3.2.1 Step 4 for a list of possible chemical analysis methodologies available. If a standard method is not available, an analytical method may be developed for a particular situation. Facilities that may be approached are research institutions, higher education institutions and even some testing laboratories.

        • (b) The sample analysis should be done either by a laboratory that is accredited (accredited to chemically analyse the specific pollutant/s) in terms of SABS ISO/IEC 17025 of the South African National Accreditation System (SANAS), or a laboratory that is participating in a proficiency testing programme and is in a process of obtaining SANAS accreditation, which latter laboratory must produce a proficiency test certificate to the client before doing any sample analysis.

          Visit the South African National Accreditation System (SANAS) website for a list of ISO/IEC 17025 (Testing and Calibration Laboratories) accredited facilities that may offer some of the methods required. Go to the Directory of Accredited Laboratories and select Testing Laboratories (https://www.sanas.co.za). Selecting the schedule of the facility will show the scope of accreditation i.e. for which methods the facility was deemed competent by SANAS. Record the facility’s name and accreditation number on the reporting template.

          It is important to note the following:

          When selecting a facility for an accredited service, carefully review the accreditation schedule of the facility. Confirm that the accredited service that is required is included in the scope of accreditation. Ensure that the methods are accredited for the type of samples to be submitted. For example, elemental analysis of dust is not the same method as elemental analysis in water samples. Please confirm with the facility for which type of samples that their method was validated.

          If facility is not SANAS accredited for a method, request the outcome of the last scores from the proficiency testing scheme (PTS) that the facility participates in for that particular method. Refer to the Reporting tutorial for an example on how to complete the form in the event that a facility is not accredited.

        • (c) A minimum number of samples taken as determined in accordance with 8.3.3.1 (a) of this guideline must each be individually analysed.

          Chemical analysis should be carried out as per the method specified for the pollutant. All the samples that were taken during the sampling schedule to represent the HEG should be analysed in order to report the exposure for a specific pollutant.

          When the chemical analysis result is below the analytical limit of detection of the method, the laboratory will report that the result below detectable limit (BDL). One recommendation is that the BDL value is used in the determination of exposure and further calculations.

          It is important that the manner in which BDL value are treated, is clearly documented and, for institutional memory, the reasoning behind the BDL approach should be explained.

      5. Gravimetric Weighing Facilities

        The COP should contain measures to ensure that gravimetric weighing facilities have a quality control programme in place. (Refer to Annexure E for information on the requirements for a weighing facility and to Annexure F for a quality control programme. Annexures E and F are for information purposes only.)

        Although SANAS accreditation is not required for gravimetric weighing, strict quality control procedures should be implemented in the weighing facility. A significant amount of uncertainty in the personal exposure assessment may be attributed to poor quality control during weighing. ISO 15767 “Workplace atmospheres — Controlling and characterising uncertainty in weighing collected aerosols” is a useful standard to follow for a weighing facility. Other standard methodologies may also be referenced such as MDHS 14/4 and NIOSH 0600.

          A few factors are highlighted for consideration in a weighing facility:
        • Control the humidity so that it remains stable to within ± 2% of a set humidity (range of ± 45 – 50%).
        • Control the temperature in the room so that it remains stable to within ± 1°C of a set temperature (range of ± 20 – 25 °C).
        • Eliminate vibrations to the table on which the electronic balance is standing to prevent fluctuations in the readings obtained.
        • Eliminate static electricity (static mats or a static ioniser).
        • Eliminate wind drafts to the balance.
        • Ensure positive air flow in the weighing room i.e. fresh air flowing from the air conditioning unit out into air ducts.
        • Prevent contamination of clean and sampled filters.

        It is recommended that the Quality System is aligned with the organisational quality system. It is important to note that some technical aspects related to the personal monitoring may not be covered by the organisations quality system.

          Some quality systems are discussed below:
        • ISO 9001 Certification: A quality system that requires policies and procedures to be in place to ensure that organisational practices are consistently executed to a high standard. This certification ensures that products and services are comparable with internationally recognised practices. Most mining companies may already have ISO 9001 Certification (and others) in place. ISO 9001 is systems orientated and compliance based i.e. have a system and follow it. What this means is that exposure assessment results may be produced according to a set of procedures – but there may be no measure of the integrity of the outcomes.
        • ISO 17025 Accreditation: A quality system that requires policies, procedures and records to assure that valid and reliable results are consistently produced. This quality system is technology orientated and competency based i.e. have a system, follow it and have proof of the integrity of results. What this means is that there is a system to ensure that valid, accurate and reliable results are produced by the weighing facility, for example. This system applies to testing and calibration facilities however, the management and technical requirements may be useful to the mine.
        • ISO 17020 Accreditation: A quality system that requires policies, procedures and records to assure that impartial and valid assessments are carried out in compliance with a standard or regulation. This system is similar to ISO 17025 but have additional requirements for procedures where an inspection is carried out against legislative standards/regulations. For the purposes of this guideline, the assessment will be personal exposure to airborne pollutants in compliance with national regulations. The requirements of the standard will assist the user to assess and manage the aspects that may have a negative impact on the quality of exposure assessment results.

        The accreditation standards can be used as a guideline to develop procedures and implement the standards to assure the quality of exposure assessment results. An organisation may consider applying for accreditation with the South African National Accreditation System (SANAS) at a later stage or if deemed necessary by the mine management.

    4. Personal exposure monitoring
        The COP should set out measures to ensure that personal exposure monitoring is conducted to obtain reliable estimates of employee shift exposures in a workplace. Samples taken should be:
      • (a) Representative of all full working shifts;
      • (b) Measured and compared to occupational exposure limits per working shifts e.g. day shift and night shift;
      • (c) Randomly be spread over the full sampling cycle period for each HEG; and
      • (d) Randomly be spread across all occupations within the HEG.

      The COP should document the duration of shift cycles and be updated as changes are made in these cycles. It is important to realise that when shifts become longer (i.e. from 8 to 12 hours) that employees are exposed to airborne pollutants for longer and the subsequent exposure concentrations may be higher. Due to the operational activities the exposures may differ between day and night shift. The aim should be to obtain reliable concentrations employee exposures in all shifts and take samples that represent the HEG.

      1. Training

        The COP should set out the training programme in place for:

        When ISO 17020 and/or 17025 is used as a guideline to establish an occupational hygiene quality system, the training requirements will be easy to identify. Each link in the chain of events to assess the personal exposure of employees to airborne pollutants will require specific competencies from each individual in that chain. In this context “competency” can be described as the ability to carry out an action to ensure that the integrity and quality of the exposure assessment is maintained. Training requirements may be included in the organisation’s performance evaluation process.

        Training should address areas where a lack of knowledge exists and should aim to standardize practices. Inconsistencies in the execution of procedures will harm the integrity of the exposure assessment process.

        It is important to document how training planned and executed when it is not under the direct control of the mine. For example, what is the criteria of the facility chosen for training, how will assessments be carried out and what is the expectation from the mine in terms of the outcome of the training,

        • (a) Storage and maintenance of equipment;

          Refer to the instrument and equipment manuals for instructions on how to handle sampling equipment. Training should be aligned with these manuals.

        • (b) Issuing of the sampling equipment;

          Training on the practical issuing of sampling equipment and may cover the how, who and when to issue:

          How: Handed over to the subject vs. attached as required
          Who: Correct employee is sampled as per the sampling strategy
          When: Start vs. during the shift.

        • (c) Wearer of the sampling equipment;

          Depending on the number of employees in the organisation, the training of the wearer of equipment may become very complex. It may be worthwhile to develop short induction/training sheets for each type of sampling. The issuer may explain the sampling and highlight critical aspects that may have an adverse effect on the quality of the data i.e. the sorbent tubes should not be submerged in water. A copy of the training sheet may then be issued to the wearer for reference. Alternatively, a ‘shadow’ may be appointed to follow the wearer and make sure that sampling is carried out according to plan. It may be sensible to find a workable solution within the organisation through the Health and Safety Structure of the mine.

        • (d) Handling of the sampling equipment;

          Refer to the instruction manual for guidelines on how to handle sampling equipment and instrumentation. Sampling and analysis methodologies may provide guidance to the handling of sampling media. Training should include both aspects when it comes to the handling of sampling equipment.

          It is important to document how sampling equipment is handled at the mine to ensure accuracy and consistency during the sampling process.

        • (e) Transportation of equipment; and

          The manner in which equipment is transported depends on the operations of a mine. In some cases sampling equipment may be handled by hand and in some cases, there is no other alternative but to make use of a courier. Training should be specific and focused on areas that will impact on the quality and integrity of the samples. Specify the requirements in the service contract when the transportation of sampling equipment is done by a courier i.e. handle with care, keep cold, deliver within in 12 hours etc. It is important to document how sampling equipment is transported at the mine to ensure that the integrity of the sample is protected.

        • (f) Pre and post calibration of the sampling train checks.

          Please refer to ISO 13137 “Workplace atmospheres — Pumps for personal sampling of chemical and biological agents — Requirements and test methods” for guidance on what to include in the training.

    5. Hierarchy of control

      In this Section the user is required to describe the different control measures that are implemented. This information should be maintained and updated as circumstances change. This information will be valuable when the exposure measurement data is evaluated.

      When reporting to the DMR an explanation should be given for over-exposures. It is not sufficient to only state that a control failed. The root cause of the over-exposure should be reported and the corrective action that was taken. During the next reporting a follow-up should be reported. It is suggested that this explanation is done in the form of a summary table similar to a high-level 11.5 investigation report.

      To determine the root cause of the over-exposure, start with a simple process of asking the question “Why?” until the root cause is determined. The corrective action should address the root cause in order to prevent the over-exposure from occurring again. If the same type of control failed in two different HEGs it does not necessarily mean that the root cause is the same.

        The COP should address the hierarchy of controls which are going to be implemented to mitigate the risk/s. The following hierarchy of controls should be taken into consideration:
      • Elimination;
      • Engineering controls;
      • Administrative controls;
      • Minimising (the risk); and
      • Personal protective equipment (PPE).

      It is suggested that a table is included in the COP that describes the different controls that have been implemented for each pollutant that was identified e.g. the engineering control for one pollutant may be different for another pollutant. On investigating the failure of controls one might find that one control is more effective on one pollutant than on another.

    6. Recording and Reporting
        The COP should address the following:
      1. Record keeping system
          A record keeping system, which records the exposure history of each HEG at the mine, should be kept and be readily available at the mine, including any reasons for deviation on sample results such as:
        • (a) Controls not operating effectively; and
        • (b) Events or factors which have influenced the results, e.g. air sampling at surface operations after high rainfall or during excessive winds.

        A quality control system as described in Section 8.3.3.5 will assist the user in having comprehensive records readily available i.e. the user must be able to access historical data when requested by a DMR Inspector during an audit. Readily available means that a document should be retrieved within an hour from the initial request. It is recommended to have a document registry and maintain it as new documents are created.

        Detailed records are important when trying to find the relationship between personal exposure measurements and the components of the risk assessment or changing circumstances. The integration of information from the different departments and/or disciplines at the mine, may assist the user to evaluate and interpret personal exposure measurements.

      2. Mandatory reports

        Reporting to the DMR must be done on a quarterly basis for all categories, that is A, B, C and D. (Refer to Annexure C: Classification bands and reporting forms. Annexure C is mandatory and must be complied with.)

          NOTE:
        • (1) For all categories A, B and C are the measurement results on the samples already taken at that time at the end of that quarter. The rolling average results must be reported at the end of each sampling cycle.

          Results should be reported per quarter and independently from other quarters. The 90th percentile for a pollutant for the quarter is then calculated and reported on the form.

        • (2) The results in a HEG of which 90th percentile equates to less than 10% of the OEL will be classified under “D category.”

          It is important to document how D Category HEGs are monitored and controlled. Reporting to the on the D Category HEGs are not required. However, maintain records (i.e. portfolio evidence) of the monitoring results in the event that the exposure changes and/or when the DMR would like to review the records.

        • (3) Pollutant(s) that do not have an additive effect on the target organ(s) must be separately reported.

          Pollutants that do not have an additive effect with other pollutants should be reported as using the single pollutant reporting form. Confirm with the relevant Occupational Medical Practitioner (OMP) first before making the assumption that pollutants are additive or not. Even though pollutants may have a negative impact on the same organ, the mechanisms of attack may be different from one another.

  4. Occupational Medical Surveillance

    It is important that the relevant OMP contributes to the documentation of this section of the COP.

    There is currently not a standard method that provides guidance on how to link medical surveillance data with occupational hygiene measurements. At best, exposure measurement concentrations for an individual can be linked to his/her medical records via the Human Resources system using a unique identifier such as the employee’s national identification number, passport number or company staff number.

    One example of linking exposure to the medical surveillance records is to create a database for each exposed employee that contains the personal nr, pollutant, exposure concentrations, type of and the values obtained during the medical surveillance. Find a pragmatic way to record and store the information for long-term safekeeping. When the employee leaves the employ of the mine, this information has to be summarised on the DMR 275 Record of Hazardous Work form.
